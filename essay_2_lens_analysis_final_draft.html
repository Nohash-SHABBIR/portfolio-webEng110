Essay 2: Lens Analysis **FINAL DRAFT**

In "Sponsors of Literacy," Deborah Brandt discusses how various individuals and organizations influence our literacy development. Brandt suggests that literacy extends beyond skills; it’s shaped by culture, relationships, and the resources available to us. This concept closely relates to Amy Tan’s essay "Mother Tongue," where she reflects on her experiences with language and identity in light of her mother’s "limited English." Examining Tan’s story alongside my own experiences as a non-native English speaker learning digital skills, it becomes clear that sponsors impact our learning in both supportive and challenging ways. This analysis extends Brandt’s ideas by showing that, while some sponsors help us grow, others create obstacles—yet we can still navigate our learning paths with agency. Tan’s "Mother Tongue" illustrates how sponsors influence literacy in unique ways. She describes her relationship with language, particularly the impact her mother’s limited English had on her sense of self and approach to writing. While her mother might not be a typical sponsor, she indirectly influenced Tan’s literacy by encouraging her to engage deeply with language despite its difficulties. Tan shares, “I felt that I had to write in a different way from the way I was thinking” (Tan), illustrating the tension she felt between her family’s language and societal expectations. Teachers and cultural standards also shaped Tan’s English development, at times making her feel inadequate even as her abilities grew. This highlights how societal views on language can support or undermine confidence, influencing our approach to communication. In my case, I faced similar hurdles learning English and adapting to a new culture after moving to an English-speaking country. The language barrier was just one challenge; understanding cultural norms and daily life was equally demanding. My parents, like Tan’s mother, were supportive but couldn’t guide me fully due to their own language and cultural limitations. However, in college, I found direct sponsors, such as teachers and classmates, who helped me build confidence and provided resources for learning. For example, some of my college teachers encouraged me to practice writing and gave valuable feedback, supporting both my English literacy and digital skills. Friends I made in computer classes also introduced me to tools that helped me develop my technical skills. However, as with Tan, I occasionally faced skepticism from peers who questioned my abilities due to my language background. This experience reflects Brandt’s idea of sponsorship, where certain figures control or influence access to literacy. Brandt explains that “sponsors…lend their resources or credibility. But can also regulate the literacy of others” (Brandt 167). My sponsors empowered me to grow, while others unintentionally hindered my progress, highlighting the mixed impact sponsors have on learning. In conclusion, by comparing Tan’s experiences to my journey as a non-native English speaker, we see how Brandt’s concepts on literacy sponsorship are evident in real life. Both Tan and I show how sponsors shape our literacy journeys, revealing both the positive and negative sides of this influence. Understanding the varied impact of sponsors, from family to educators to peers, expands Brandt’s theory to show how people can both support and challenge our growth in different types of literacy.
